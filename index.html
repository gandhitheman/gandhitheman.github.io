The way of Gandhi

Gandhi the Man
How one man changed himself to change the world
Eknath Easwaran

Gandhi is perhaps the most important figure in the history of nonviolent transformation, but what he actually taught is greatly misunderstood. This book tells the human story of how Gandhi, once a meek and unremarkable Indian lawyer, came to lead some of the greatest social justice movements of all time, including the removal of a colonial occupying power without a war (a true first in history), and became a central influence for nonviolent leaders from Martin Luther King Jr. to Nelson Mandela.

Any person who wishes to truly do more to stop the spread of evil, destruction, and injustice in the world, would do well to read this book. For it is the story of the power not of any specific political action---which is what we usually think of when it comes to Gandhi---but of one's state of mind and spirit. When one's spirit is grounded and one's mind is balanced (which is no small feat), then whatever action that must be taken to change the world becomes clear, natural, and empowered beyond imagination by one's inner wholeness and connection with the universe.

The story of Badshah Khan

Nonviolent soldier of Islam
Badshah Khan: A man to match his mountains
Eknath Easwaran

The story of the revolutionary transformation of a violent, militant Islamic group, themselves subjected to "mass shootings, torture, the destruction of their fields and homes, jail, flogging and humiliations", into a people that set down their arms and stood nonviolently against their opponents, playing a key role in the dismantling of their colonial oppression.

This tranformation was led by Abdul Ghaffar Khan, later known as Badshah Khan (King of Khans), who through an intense study of the Qur'an during his imprisonment came to realize the deepest meaning of Islam as a religion of love, compassion, and oneness of the universe, indistinguishable in its fundamental faith from that of all world religions---a faith with the power to change the entire world through love and the empowerment of human action.

If you would like to undertake one concrete act to truly help the situation in the Middle East, from Gaza to Iran, read this book with an open mind and an open heart. One of the most powerful forces in the world is narrative, and raising the deepest narratives of nonviolent transformation into our collective consciousness, especially in the modern context of Islam and conflicts of faith, is one of the most important things that any individual on Earth can do to help.

Martin Luther King Jr.'s six principles of nonviolence

  1. Nonviolence can overcome evil, but nonviolence requires tremendous courage and is not for the faint of heart.
  2. Nonviolence seeks not to punish and humiliate the opponent but to win their friendship and understanding.
  3. Evil itself should be opposed, not the people committing evil acts.
  4. The true nonviolent actor is willing to accept suffering, including imprisonment, without retaliation, knowing that such trials lead to growth of the soul.
  5. Nonviolence avoids not only external violence but internal violence of the mind and spirit as well. The true nonviolent activist not only refuses to physically harm their opponent, but also refuses to hate them no matter how terrible their deeds.
  6. One must have a deep faith in the future, stemming from the wholehearted belief that the universe is on the side of justice.

Some of Gandhi's principles

"It is not nonviolence if we merely love those that love us. It is nonviolence only when we love those that hate us. I know how difficult it is to follow this grand law of love. But are not all great and good things difficult to do?"

"Disobedience to be civil must be sincere, respectful, restrained, never defiant, must be based upon some well-understood principle, must not be capricious and, above all, must have no ill will or hatred behind it."

"I have not the shadow of a doubt that any man or woman can achieve what I have, if he or she would make the same effort and cultivate the same hope and faith."

"One man cannot do right in one department of life whilst he is occupied in doing wrong in any other department. Life is one indivisible whole."

"I hate the system of the government that the British people have set up in India. I hate the ruthless exploitation of India even as I hate from the bottom of my heart the hideous system of untouchability for which millions of Hindus have made themselves responsible. But I do not hate the domineering Englishmen as I refuse to hate the domineering Hindus. I seek to reform them in all the loving ways that are open to me. My noncooperation has its roots not in hatred, but in love."

"I have learnt through bitter experience the one supreme lesson to conserve my anger, and as heat conserved is transmuted into energy, even so our anger controlled can be transmuted into a power which can move the world."

"I believe that if one man gains spiritually the whole world gains with him."

Frequently asked questions

Won't love and compassion for an evil opponent create more space for evil?

To support someone's terrible deeds is not true love or support for the person. Love, compassion, support---true nonviolence---means accepting the actor independently of their evil deeds, and taking skillfull action to not only block their deeds but to rescue and transform their spirit, for they too suffer greatly for the harm they cause to others. To block their deeds without any consideration of their spirit can lead only to short-term change. To categorize a whole person as an evildoer that must be done away with, no matter how terrible their actions, is never fundamentally necessary and in many cases leads instead to the reinforcement and empowerment of so-called evildoers who gather into their own communities to protect themselves from their attackers.

Even in times of crisis when a great intervention is required to prevent someone from causing harm, the identical outward intervention can be done in different states of mind, one far less helpful than the other. One can view the other as a terrible person that must be punished for their deeds, with such punishment justified as long as the one calling for the punishment is on the side of good. Alternatively, one can view the other as a wayward family member, overtaken by intense sociopsychological powers that have caused them to spiral into outwardly destructive behaviors, but who is ultimately a lost soul in need of just as much, if not more compassion, as all other suffering beings, even if it is crucial to stop their immediate harmful actions.

Thus any action taken against an evil deed can be done with love for the actor committing the evil deed, with compassion and love for the part of the actor's soul, however hidden it may be, that has not yet been taken by evil and which ultimately carries the power to transform them totally if accepted and nurtured.

"For a nonviolent person, the whole world is one family. They will thus fear none, nor will others fear them." - Gandhi

Didn't Gandhi, MLK Jr., and other nonviolent leaders do and say some problematic things too?

Perhaps. But to therefore abandon their teachings, or the teachings of anyone who has performed problematic actions, is a great mistake. Isn't it also an option to learn from these people, including their failures, and to subsequently do even greater things in this world?

Gandhi, Khan, and MLK Jr.'s movements were powered by religious faith. Is there a version of nonviolence that does not require such faith? In fact, wouldn't this ultimately be better, given how many problems religion seems to cause?

The faith of those who have overcome great evils in the world, although often articulated with specific religious images, is not blind faith in a kingly God ruling over his kingdom or in deities with supernatural powers. The faith of Gandhi, Khan, and MLK Jr. was a faith in the wholeness of the universe, of our inseparability from one another, of the validity and tremendous power of the perspective that we are all one. It is a faith that attacking others, even in one's own mind, has no greater utility than one's left hand attacking one's right. That called God by such great persons is completely independent of religious specifics. When Gandhi, Khan, and MLK Jr. spoke of God, though they came from three different backgrounds (Hinduism, Islam, and Christianity), they were speaking of the same thing---the wholeness of the universe and power of love to overcome evil.

To channel such faith to change the world requires no religious affiliation or upbringing. It comes from a deep investigation of one's own nature and a natural curiosity for the possibility that there is far more connecting us all than separating us.

Most nonviolent protests these days, even if they generate important moments of solidarity, seem to barely move the needle in the long run. Isn't it time to try something else besides the ways of Gandhi and MLK Jr.?

According to both Gandhi and King, nonviolent action is not about protesting, nor about raising awareness and visibility in front of the media, community, voters, and legislators, which is so often seen as the goals of modern political demonstrations. Demonstrating, donating, voting are but a tiny fraction of the actions one can take to truly effect nonviolent change. Moreover, if these actions are taken with even the slightest ill will toward the opponent, they are not nonviolent in the way conceived of by Gandhi, Khan and King, and therefore far less powerful than the same actions taken with no ill will at all. For actions leave impressions in the mind (called "samskaras" in ancient Indian philosophy and "traces" in modern neuroscience), which are shaped by the state of mind when the action is taken. Taking the same action with ill will vs love for the opponent as one's wayward sibiling leave different impressions, which subsequently come to reinforce future thoughts and actions. Impressions created in a state of mind of ill will reinforce states of mind colored by ill will. Impressions created in a state of love and compassion and a sense of unity of the whole universe reinforce those states of mind, which empower future actions far more than any sense of ill will.

To truly follow the ways of Gandhi, Khan, and King has nothing fundamentally to do with outward civil disobedience and demonstrations against injustice, although these have been a historic signature of nonviolence. To truly follow the deepest teachings of nonviolence means to remove ill will from all action, even those taken against tyrannical oppression. It means to recognize the entire world, and the entire universe, as one organism of which we are all part, no matter how extreme some of the destruction in it may be, and empower oneself with this knowledge and perspective to attain a true fearlessness through the embodiment of love. It means to set aside serious time in one's life for deep introspection and open-minded examination of all of the wild creatures in one's psyche that may get in the way of authentic nonviolence, accept them as part of one's own nature and the wholeness of the universe, and to integrate them into one's being while adhering with courage to the fundamental belief that what will remain is not violence but harmony and love, together with the power and will to transform the world for good.

For when one's spirit is grounded and invincible, everything else becomes easy. Then one becomes truly empowered, not only to be part of group protests and donations, but to undertake the far longer, quieter, but ultimately all the more powerful actions of giving one's whole self to love and compassion for the world, and to spend years learning specific skills aligned one's individual personality and inclinations that will go infinitely further towards one's own transformation and that of the world.
