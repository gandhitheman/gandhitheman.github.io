Gandhi and Ahimsa

What makes some movements for humanity's greater good more effective than others? To answer this question there is perhaps no better case study to turn to than Gandhi. Our modern approach toward social change can be largely traced to the sit-ins and other nonviolent ways championed by Gandhi during the movements he led in South Africa and India, which ultimately led to the freeing of India from British rule via a route relatively free of violent conflict. Gandhi also had a tremendous global influence, directly inspiring leaders of foundational civil rights movements. Martin Luther King Jr described Gandhi's philosophy as "the only morally and practically sound method open to oppressed people in their struggle for freedom," and adopted a powerful notion of nonviolence (ahimsa) as a guiding light of the U.S. Civil Rights movement. In its original sanskrit, Ahimsa is described as nonviolence in thought, word, or deed. Thus, true nonviolence is not only absence of physical force but even of negative thoughts about one's opponent. In Gandhi's spiritual system this follows from grasping the unified nature of all of reality, the realization that while we may often think of ourselves as separate entities it is just as valid to think of ourselves as one. From the latter perspective it does not even make any sense to think poorly of another, no matter how unhinged their actions and ideas may be. King, too, understood this profound principle that even internal violence must be avoided, not only for peace of mind but for effective action toward justice: "The nonviolent resister not only refuses to shoot his opponent but he also refuses to hate him."

Hindu concepts relevant to Gandhi's spirituality

Atman

Yoga. Yoga is not only the way of poses. In the yoga sutras Patanjali (ca. 500 BCE) defines yoga as the cessation of the movements of the "mind-stuff" (1.ii)

Karmayogi. A yogi that acts in the world.

Moksha. Nirvana or enlightenment. A state of mind in which one is liberated from the illusion of the separateness of things and in which one's understanding of the universe becomes unclouded.

Sakama and nishkama. In Hinduism all actions can be performed in one of two states of mind. Either with desire (sakama) or without desire (nishkama). In the latter all action flows not with the goal of reaping reward, as we so often motivate ourselves in our lives, but like a piece of music, freely, spontaneously, compassionately without any goal or end but as part of the unified flow of nature. One of the ultimate goals of meditation is to perform all actions in a state of nishkama, which frees us from the emotional fluctuations caused by the illusions of loss and gain and sets in harmony with our true nature.
